Research

Job Market Paper

  • Graduates in a Cycle: the effect of business cycle trajectories on labor marlet outcomes of college graduates — draft soon!

    Abstract: This paper separates the long-term effect of graduating during a recession into two distinct components: selection into college during economic downturns and labor market conditions after graduation. Using CPS data from 1976 - 2024, I show that individuals who entered college during recessions are negatively selected compared to those who enrolled in better times, and account for a larger share of the negative scarring effects typically attributed to graduation-year conditions: while for people graduated into the recession the effect of adverse labor market conditions disappears after 3 years, it remains for a decade for people enrolled in college during the recession. I also find that labor market conditions in the years immediately following graduation have a stronger impact on long-term earnings than the unemployment rate at the time of graduation accounting for additional 2% difference in average earnings over 10 years after graduation. These findings highlight the importance of accounting for both college-entry and early-career environments when evaluating the impact of graduating during a recession.

Working Papers

  • Competition and the Gender Pay Gap: Evidence from Russian Trade Withdrawal — PDF

    Abstract: In 2014, Russia imposed an import embargo on selected goods, effectively reducing trade flows and decreasing import competition across several industries. In this paper, I analyze the effect of this decrease in product market competition on the gender pay gap in Russia. This research complements the body of evidence which implies that increases in product market competition lead to lower gender pay gaps. The empirical analysis relates 2011-2019 industry-specific gender wage gaps estimated from the Russian Longitudinal Monitoring Survey to the industry-level evolution of import penetration. The results show that a 10 percentage point reduction in import competition leads to an increase of about 4 percentage points in the gender pay gap. This increase is smaller in magnitude than the effect found in the US but larger than that observed in some Eastern European countries. This phenomenon partly corresponds to an exacerbation of the gender employment gap driven primarily by high-skilled women leaving industries where import shares, and consequently competition, declined.
